Fundamentals of Investing

 

This chapter begins by stressing the importance of preparing for an investment program. Then, it provides an overview of how the factors of safety, risk, income, growth, and liquidity affect investment programs. Next, it examines the different investment alternatives available to individuals, as well as describing methods that investors can use to reduce investment risk. Included in this section on reducing risks is a discussion on the role of financial planners and material on the importance of the individual’s role in the investment process. Finally, various sources of investment information is described.

 

PREPARING FOR AN INVESTMENT PROGRAM (p. 294)

 

The decision to start an investment program is an important first step to accomplishing your long-term financial goals.

 

˜     Establishing Investment Goals

 

•     To be useful, investment goals (as all goals) must be specific and measurable.

 

•     Investment goals must be tailored to your particular financial needs.

 

•     Investment goals must always be oriented toward the future.

 

˜     Performing a Financial Checkup

 

•      Before beginning an investment program, you must make sure your personal financial affairs are in order.

 

To be successful, you must:

 

1.     Work to balance your budget.

 

2.     Obtain adequate insurance protection.

 

3.     Start an emergency fund equal to three to nine months’ living expenses.

 

4.     Have access to other sources of cash for emergency needs.

 

˜      Getting the Money Needed to Start an Investment Program

 

•        Once you have established your investment goals and completed your personal financial checkup, it’s time to start investing—assuming that you have enough money to finance your investments.

 

•        The following suggestions may help you obtain the money you need.

 

1.       You must pay yourself first.

 

2.       Take advantage of employer-sponsored retirement programs.

 

3.       You should participate in an elective savings program.

 

4.       You can also make a special savings effort one or two months each year.

 

5.       You should take advantage of gifts, inheritances, and windfalls.

 

FACTORS AFFECTING THE CHOICE OF INVESTMENTS (p. 299)

 

Although each investor may have specific individual reasons for investing, there are a number of factors that all investors must consider.

 

˜      Safety and Risk

 

The safety and risk factors are two sides of the same coin. Safety in an investment means minimal risk of loss. On the other hand, risk in an investment means a measure of uncertainty about the outcome.

 

A speculative investmen t is a high risk investment made in the hope of earning a relatively large profit in a short period of time

 

Investments range from very safe to very risky. At one end of the investment spectrum are very safe investments that attract conservative investors, e.g., Canada Savings Bonds. Investors pick such investments because they know there is very little chance that investments will become worthless or that they will lose any money.

 

At the other end of the investment spectrum are very risky investments. Such investments offer the possibility of a larger dollar return, but if they are unsuccessful, the investor may lose most or all of the initial investment, e.g., Bre-X Resources. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bre-X

 

˜      Risk Tolerance

 

Risk tolerance is the amount of psychological pain you are willing to suffer from your investments. You could lose part or your entire principal, the purchasing power of your investment can decrease and you may not receive the returns you expected.

 

In addition, the money you invested in securities, mutual funds, and other similar investments are not insured by the CDIC (Canadian Deposit Insurance Corporation, http://www.cdic.ca).

 

Components of the Risk Factor

 

The factor of risk associated with a specific investment does change from time to time. In fact, the overall risk factor can be broken down into the following five components.

 

1.       Business risk

 

o         the risk associated with bad management, unsuccessful products, competition, higher input costs, lower output prices, costly technological

 

changes, etc.

 

o         this risk is faced with common and preferred share investments and investments in corporate bonds.

 

2.       Inflation risk

 

o         this is also called purchasing power risk – the risk that when you do get your money back from investments, items that you purchase will be more expensive due to higher inflation.

 

3.       Interest rate risk

 

o         if interest rates rise, the present value of income streams from investments declines, e.g., if rates rise, bond prices fall; of course, if rates fall, bond prices rise.

 

4.       Market risk

 

o          all markets rise and fall over time, sometimes as a result of economic cycles and sometimes due to changing investor sentiment.

 

5.       Global Investment Risk

 

o          in addition to facing all the risks mentioned above, investing in other than North American securities might entail several additional risks:

i)  Information Problems

 

o       accounting standards might not be as high in some foreign countries, so the financial statements which are the usual basis of evaluation may be less trustworthy.

ii)  Currency Risk

 

o         even though you might have found an investment which earned a good return in its local currency, when that currency gets converted back to Canadian dollars, due to exchange changes, the overall

 

investment might have lost value

 

o         from time to time, some foreign countries have put exchange controls into place, so that it might not even be possible to repatriate the funds easily.

iii)  Withholding Taxes

 

o          often, returns on foreign investments will have taxed deducted in the foreign country.

 

When this takes place, you end up paying tax only once (if Canada has a tax treaty with that foreign country). The tax rate will be at whichever country has the highest tax rate. For example, if the foreign country has a lower rate of tax than Canada, you pay the foreign tax, then have to “top it up” to the Canadian level; on the other hand, if the Canadian rate is higher, you get a tax credit for the foreign taxes paid, but still end up paying all of the Canadian tax.

Investment Income and Growth (p. 303)

 

Investors sometimes purchase certain investments because they want a predictable return or distribution of income from their investment.

 

o            If income is a primary objective, most investors choose government bonds, corporate bonds, preferred stock, utility stocks, or selected common stocks. Other investments that provide income potential are mutual funds and real estate rental property.

 

o            Growth is more likely from common stock and mutual funds but could also arise with bonds (due to interest rate decreases).

 

o          keep in mind the alternative tax treatment that exists between interest (from bonds), dividends (from stock and mutual funds) and capital gains (more likely in stock and mutual funds, but also possible with bonds).

 

o            The more speculative investments, such as commodities, options, precious metals, gemstones, and collectibles, offer little, if any potential for regular income.

 

-             these assets have much more risk and less predictable growth possibilities.

 

-             from a tax standpoint, capital gains (or losses) would prevail.

 

Investment Liquidity (p. 304)

 

Liquidity is the ease with which an asset can be converted to cash without a substantial loss in dollar value.

 

AN OVERVIEW OF INVESTMENT ALTERNATIVES (p. 305)

 

When establishing an investment program, investors should begin by gathering as much information as possible about investment alternatives.

 

Once you have established your emergency fund and have some money in the bank, it’s time to consider other investment alternatives.

 

˜          Stock or Equity Financing (p. 305)

 

Equity capital is money that a business obtains from its owners.

 

Investors should consider at least two factors before investing in stock:

 

1.       A corporation is not obligated to repay the money obtained from the sale of stock or to repurchase the stock at a later date.

2.       A corporation is under no legal obligation to pay dividends.

 

A dividend is a distribution of money, stocks, or other property that a corporation pays to shareholders.

There are two types of stock—common and preferred.

 

o Preferred Shares

 

A security that shows ownership in a corporation and gives the holder a claim, prior to the claim of common stockholders, on earnings and also generally on assets (to a fixed extent) in the event of liquidation.

 

o Common Shares

 

Common shareholders are “residual claimants” on the company’s assets and earnings, which means they are last-in-line to receive any distributions. This could be a bad thing if there is little available for distribution, but might be a good thing if there is much available for distribution.

 

˜          Corporate and Government Bonds (p. 306)

 

There are two types of bonds that an investor might consider.

 

o Government Bonds

 

A government bond is the written pledge of a government or a municipality that it will repay a specified sum of money with interest over time until some maturity date.

 

o Corporate Bonds

 

A corporate bond is a corporation’s written pledge that it will repay a specified amount of money with interest over time until some maturity date.

 

The maturity dates for most bonds range between 1 and 30 years.

 

Interest payments on bonds are made every six months, on the anniversary date and semi-anniversary date of the issue.

 

The risk differential between these types of bonds can be substantial, and can even differ quite a bit between various bonds within a given type.

 

o              Government of Canada bonds have no default risk since the government can both print money and tax residents; the main risk one has with such bonds is interest rate risk (rates might rise and bond prices fall).

 

o               Provincial bonds are riskier than Government of Canada bonds, since provinces can’t print money, but can only tax their residents - if tax rates are too high, those that can (individuals and corporations) might simply move to a lower taxed province.

 

o               Municipal bonds are riskier than provincial bonds as all municipalities can do to generate revenue is tax local residents - higher taxes means individuals and

 

corporations would move elsewhere, perhaps to neighboring cities, but the municipality might still be left with welfare costs, etc.

 

o               Corporate bonds are even riskier than any government bond, as the ability to service corporate debt depends on the company’s fortunes in selling its product or services.

 

˜          Mutual Funds (p. 306)

 

A mutual fund is an investment alternative available to individuals who pool their money to buy stocks, bonds, and other securities based on the selections of professional managers who work for an investment company.

 

Professional management is an especially important factor. Another reason why investors choose mutual funds is because of diversification - they get to hold a portfolio of a wide variety of securities with very low unit cost.

 

The goals of one investor may differ from those of another investor. The managers of mutual funds realize this and tailor programs to meet individual needs and objectives – just look in the business section of the newspaper and see the large number of mutual funds catering to all types of investment goals with different classes of assets and mixtures thereof.

 

˜          Segregated Funds (p. 307)

 

A segregated fund is an investment alternative in the form of an annuity that is similar to a mutual fund but that is less risky as it provides a certain degree of insurance to the investor.

 

o          once the annuity matures (in at least 10 years), the investor is guaranteed up to 100 percent of the initial investment minus any withdrawals.

 

o          should the person die before the maturity guarantee, a death benefit equal to the original investment is provided – hence like a mutual fund with an insurance

 

feature.

 

o          from an estate planning standpoint, since in the event of the death of the annuitant, the death benefit of a “seg fund” (as they are often called) passes directly to a beneficiary and not the estate, the proceeds are not subject to any probate fees or creditor attachment.

 

o        sale prior to the maturity could be at a price higher, lower or equal to the investment.

 

o              while it might sound attractive to “get your money back for sure” in ten 10 years, it would not be a very good investment outcome (although better than losing money over the 10 years).

•               If you were to invest $100 today and get back $100 in 10 years, the return on investment would be found from the following:

$100 = $100 x PV(10y, K%)

 

Solving for K, you find its 0%, i.e., you haven’t earned anything.

 

•               but the problem is even worse than that, because of inflation over the time frame – if inflation was 2% each and every year over the 10 years,

 

$100 would grow to     $100 x FV(10y, 2%) = $121.89

 

This means that 10 years later it would take $121.89 to buy the same basket of goods which you could have purchased for $100 at the earlier time. You get your $100 back through the guarantee, but it doesn’t provide you with enough to purchase the now more expensive basket.

 

In fact, in real terms, you have been losing 2% per year to inflation – hence you are not really “breaking even” once inflation is factored into the investment.

 

˜          Real Estate (p. 307)

 

As a rule, real estate increases in value and eventually sells for a profit, but there are no guarantees.

 

Any investment has its disadvantages, and real estate is no exception. Like any other investment, real estate must be evaluated - this usually requires professional assistance, and valuations by different valuators can result in substantially different estimated values.

 

One very attractive feature about real estate used as a home is the principal residence exemption from capital gains - no tax on increased values of your principal residence. Keep in mind that if the principal residence declines in value during the period over which you hold it, the decrease in value cannot be treated as a capital loss.

 

˜          Other Investment Alternatives (p. 309)

 

A speculative investment is a high-risk investment that is undertaken in the hope of earning a relative large profit, usually in a short period of time.

 

Speculative investments include: call options, put options, derivatives, commodities, precious metals, gemstones, coins, stamps, and antiques and collectibles. More detailed information is provided in later chapters.

FACTORS THAT REDUCE INVESTMENT RISK (p. 313)

 

˜          The Role of a Financial Planner

 

•        There is no such thing as a nationally designated financial planner in Canada. A number of organizations however allow special designations after certain criteria have been met.

 

•          Financial planners may be divided into two categories;

 

o            the first category consists of salaried planners who provide advice either free or for a nominal fee.

 

o            The second category consists of commission planners who receive compensation from the sellers of the services and products that they sell.

 

•        More information about financial planners is presented in the Appendix after Chapter 1.

 

˜          Your Role in the Investment Process

 

•          Evaluation of an investment should begin before purchasing an investment and continue after it is purchased – good investors continually assess the value of their investments.

 

•          Some basic elements of evaluation include:

 

1.               Evaluate potential investments.

 

2.               Monitor the value of your investment.

 

3.               Keep accurate and current records.
